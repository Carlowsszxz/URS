What Happened in the Cavite Mutiny?

The Year 1872: A Historic Turning Point

Key Historical Event: February 17, 1872 – The execution of GOMBURZA

The year 1872 is a historic year marked by two significant events: the Cavite Mutiny and the martyrdom of the three priests Mariano Gómez, José Burgos, and Jacinto Zamora, later immortalized as GOMBURZA. These events are important milestones in Philippine history and have caused ripples throughout time, directly influencing the decisive events of the Philippine Revolution toward the end of the nineteenth century.

While their historical significance is unquestioned, what made this year controversial were the different sides to the story–a battle of perspectives supported by primary sources. In this case study, we zoom in on the events of the Cavite Mutiny, a major factor in the awakening of nationalism among Filipinos during this period.

SPANISH PERSPECTIVE

Spanish Accounts of the Cavite Mutiny

The documentation of Spanish historian José Montero y Vidal centered on the interpretation that the Cavite Mutiny was an attempt to overthrow the Spanish government in the Philippines. Although regarded as a historian, his account of the mutiny was criticized as woefully biased and rabid for a scholar.

Another account, taken from the official report of Governor-General Rafael Izquierdo, implicated the native clergy, who were then active in the movement toward the secularization of parishes. These two Spanish accounts corroborated each other in presenting the mutiny as part of a broader revolutionary conspiracy.

Primary Source: Montero y Vidal's Account

"The abolition of privileges enjoyed by the laborers of the Cavite arsenal of exemption from the tribute was, according to some, the cause of the insurrection. There were, however, other causes. Spanish revolution which overthrew a secular throne; the propaganda carried on by an unbridled press against monarchical principles... were the determining circumstances which gave rise, among certain Filipinos, to the idea of attaining their independence."

– José Montero y Vidal, Spanish Version of the Cavite Mutiny of 1872

Primary Source: Governor-General Izquierdo's Report

"It seems definite that the insurrection was motivated and prepared by the native clergy, by the mestizos and native lawyers, and by those known here as abogadillos. The instigators... protested against the injustice of the government in not paying the provinces for their tobacco crop... They encouraged the rebellion by protesting what they called the injustice of having obliged the workers in the Cavite arsenal to pay tribute."

– Rafael Izquierdo, Official Report on the Cavite Mutiny

Analysis of the Spanish Accounts

The Spanish accounts underscore the reason for the alleged "revolution": the abolition of privileges enjoyed by the workers of the Cavite arsenal, such as exemption from the payment of tribute and from polos y servicios (forced labor). They also identified other causes which made the issue appear more serious, including the involvement of the native clergy, who–out of spite against the Spanish friars–were said to have conspired and supported the rebels.

In the Spanish narrative, the events of 1872 were premeditated and part of a large conspiracy involving educated Filipinos, mestizos, lawyers, and residents of Manila and Cavite. They allegedly planned to liquidate high-ranking Spanish officers and kill the friars. The supposed signal among the conspirators was the firing of rockets from Intramuros.

FILIPINO & FOREIGN PERSPECTIVE

Differing Accounts: Filipino and Foreign Perspectives

Two other primary accounts counter the versions presented by Izquierdo and Montero. Trinidad H. Pardo de Tavera, a Filipino perspective, argued that the uprising was merely a mutiny by Filipino soldiers and laborers arising from dissatisfaction with Izquierdo's draconian policies, including the abolition of privileges and the prohibition of establishing a school of arts and trades for Filipinos.

According to Pardo de Tavera, up to that time there had been no intention of secession from Spain, and the only aspiration of the people was to secure the material and educational advancement of the country. Tavera argued that the Spanish friars and Izquierdo blew the mutiny out of proportion to address other issues, particularly the impending plans of the Spanish central government to deprive friars of power in civil governance and education.

The Martyrdom of GOMBURZA and Its Legacy

The Three Martyrs: GOMBURZA – Fathers Mariano Gómez, José Burgos & Jacinto Zamora

GOMBURZA refers to Fathers Mariano Gómez, José Burgos, and Jacinto Zamora, who were accused as the masterminds of the Cavite Mutiny. They were prominent Filipino priests charged with treason and sedition. It is believed that the Spanish clergy linked them to the mutiny to suppress the secularization movement.

They were publicly executed by garrote on February 17, 1872, a scene reportedly witnessed by a young José Rizal. Their martyrdom is widely regarded as the dawn of Philippine nationalism. Rizal later dedicated El Filibusterismo to their memory, affirming the widespread belief in their innocence and the injustice of their trial.

Conclusion: Understanding Competing Narratives

Key Takeaway: The same events told through different perspectives create fundamentally different historical narratives.

The Cavite Mutiny of 1872 demonstrates how historical events are shaped by perspective, power, and purpose. Spanish officials framed the mutiny as a revolutionary conspiracy, while Filipino and foreign observers interpreted it as an exaggerated labor dispute exploited for political and ecclesiastical ends. Understanding these competing narratives highlights the importance of critical analysis of primary sources and underscores the mutiny's role in the emergence of Philippine nationalism.
